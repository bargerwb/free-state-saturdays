What actually happens here

There's always food. People bring dishes, sometimes we cater. Somebody's kid is asleep in a stroller. Two guys in the corner are arguing about Austrian economics. You grab a plate and sit down next to someone you've never met who somehow already feels like an old friend.

The conversation is the point. Not a lecture. Not a panel. You'll talk about homeschooling, Bitcoin, that zoning board fight in Keene, what it was like to leave California, whether your kids should learn welding. Sometimes there's a speaker or an event theme. Our Super Bowl party had games, prizes, and a TPUSA halftime show. Sometimes it's just three hours of the best conversation you've had all month.

There are quiet clubhouses with aging potlucks and no kids. This isn't that. Free State Saturdays isn't run by any official organization and you don't have to be a new mover to come. It's for everyone who's already here, too.

You already know the feeling

You bite your tongue at work. You hedge what you say on the neighborhood group chat. Your Thanksgiving dinner has a list of topics everyone silently agreed to avoid. Your views aren't fringe. You just don't have a room full of people who agree yet.

Free State Saturdays is that room. The things you actually care about aren't controversial here. They're just normal.

This is for you if:

  • • You moved to NH for the Free State Project and you're still building your circle
  • • You're thinking about the move and want to see what the community actually looks like before you commit
  • • You've been here for years but your social calendar is 90% liberty events run by the same twelve people
  • • You've got kids and you're done pretending library story hour is a social life
  • • You just want to eat and talk to someone who gets it

Who shows up

Everyone. New movers who landed last week. Families who've been in NH for fifteen years. LPNH. The Free State Project. Jeremy Kauffman. The Mayor of Manchester. Tall Bill. A bunch of kids who don't care about any of those names.
